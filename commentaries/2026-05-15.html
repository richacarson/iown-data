The Session

Thursday’s record close did not survive the weekend setup. The S&P 500 fell 1.23% to 7,409.18, the Dow declined 1.07% to 49,526.16, and the Nasdaq dropped 1.55% to 26,221.52 as the bid that had carried both benchmarks through 7,500 and 50,000 was withdrawn within twenty-four hours of being established. The Russell 2000 led the decline at –2.44%; the VIX rose 5.79% to 18.26. The catalyst was not a single headline but a confluence: the 10-year Treasury yield spiked nine basis points to a fresh one-year high near 4.58%, the 30-year cleared 5%, and the 2-year broke above 4% for the first time in this cycle — the bond market repricing a Fed that may need to hike rather than cut.

The Empire State Manufacturing Index landed at 19.6 versus a 7.0 estimate, the strongest reading in more than four years, with the prices-paid sub-index surging alongside. Combined with this week’s hot CPI and PPI prints, the data argues that the inflation impulse from the energy shock is broadening rather than fading. WTI crude added more than 3% to settle near $104 and is on track for a 10% weekly gain; Brent traded above $106. The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed and the IEA continues to warn of materially undersupplied conditions through October. President Trump described the Iran ceasefire as on “massive life support” after dismissing Tehran’s latest proposal.

The Trump-Xi summit in Beijing closed without major deliverables. Claims of a 200-plane Boeing order, expanded US agricultural purchases, and an Iran-no-nukes alignment generated headlines, but no formal documentation accompanied the meeting; Beijing has separately instructed Chinese firms to hold off on H200 chip purchases pending guidance. The semiconductor complex that powered Thursday’s record close gave back ground broadly, with the rotation from AI infrastructure into defensive cash flows visible across the tape. Kevin Warsh formally took over the Federal Reserve as Chairman Powell’s term ended at the close; futures now price a 45% chance of a Fed hike before year-end, up from roughly 1% a month ago.

Underneath the headline indices, the day rewarded what it always rewards on a yield-and-oil session: balance sheets, dividends, and energy. IOWN’s Dividend Strategy declined 0.64% — less than half the S&P’s drop and notably better than DVY at –1.00%. The Growth Portfolio at –2.88% absorbed more of the rotation through its semiconductor and digital concentration. Both portfolios remain in green territory year-to-date; the Dividend sleeve leads its benchmarks meaningfully.

Defensive cash flows do their work on the days the tape needs them to. The Dividend sleeve’s 36-basis-point outperformance versus DVY on a sell-off session is exactly the architectural reason it sits where it does in the allocation. Think Like an Owner.
