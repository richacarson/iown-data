The Session

The chip-cohort divergence that defined Monday resolved decisively to the downside. A global memory selloff that began in Seoul carried straight onto Wall Street: South Korea’s Kospi fell roughly ten percent intraday and triggered exchange circuit breakers twice after regulators cautioned that the rally in leveraged chip ETFs had grown overheated. SK Hynix and Samsung Electronics each gave back more than twelve percent, and the signal traveled through every memory peer on the board.

Here at home the damage was concentrated, not broad. The Nasdaq fell 2.21% and the S&P 500 closed –1.43% at 7,365.92, yet the Dow held essentially flat at –0.09% and roughly six of every ten S&P names finished higher. The arithmetic is straightforward: technology shed better than four percent and erased the gains of nearly everything else. The VIX rose 12.21% to 19.39 — elevated, but a long way from disorder.

A second pressure point compounded the tape. A Bank of America note circulated Monday now calls for three quarter-point rate hikes in September, October, and December, a sharp reversal from its prior on-hold base case and a reading of Chair Warsh’s hawkish first meeting. The ten-year yield held near 4.50%, and the long-duration AI complex is the most sensitive seat in the house when the rate path resets higher.

This is precisely the session the dual-sleeve structure is built for. The Dividend Strategy’s ballast — consumer staples, healthcare, and defense — absorbed the blow and held the strategy to –0.59%, ahead of the S&P. The Growth Portfolio, by design concentrated in the names leading this cycle, carried the full weight of the unwind at –3.20%. Neither outcome is a surprise; both are the framework working as intended.

A crowded trade unwinding is not a thesis breaking. Carson’s SPY $645.80 support sits far below Tuesday’s close, and the buy tranches at –20/–25/–30% from the high remain untouched. We own businesses, not momentum — and we let the ballast do its job.
