The Session

The market did the work for us today. The headline indices barely moved — the S&P 500 closed essentially flat and the Nasdaq slipped roughly half a percent — but underneath that placid surface ran a rotation that rewarded exactly the kind of business we prefer to own. The Dow Jones Industrial Average climbed to within a hair of a record high, carried not by the familiar megacap technology names but by industrials, health care, and financials. The Russell 2000 added 0.71%. Breadth, not the leaderboard, told the story.

The morning’s central question had been whether Micron’s after-close print would validate the memory cycle and whether the May PCE reading would unsettle a tape already on edge. Both were answered. Micron rocketed nearly 16% on a quarter that more than quadrupled year-ago revenue, and the read-through lifted the memory and equipment complex rather than the index leaders. The PCE deflator came in at 4.1% year-over-year and 0.4% on the month — hot, the firmest annual reading since 2023, but squarely in line with what economists expected, with the monthly figure a touch below forecast. Markets treated it as the likely peak: oil has retreated roughly a third from its February highs as the Strait of Hormuz reopened, and that relief has yet to show up in the data.

So capital rotated. It moved out of the crowded megacap leaders — Apple fell nearly 6% after raising prices on MacBooks and iPads to absorb the memory-cost surge, and Nvidia and Taiwan Semiconductor gave back ground — and into the broad industrial and value complex where the Dividend Strategy lives. Caterpillar, Steel Dynamics, Paccar, Valero, and Abbott all advanced more than 3%. That is why the Dividend sleeve gained 1.63% on a day the S&P went nowhere.

FCI Tactical: A flat index is not a quiet market. The days the headline number does nothing are often the days ownership of the right businesses matters most. We were positioned for breadth, and breadth arrived.

Holdings News

Qualcomm’s data-center reframing. At its Investor Day, Qualcomm laid out a four-product roadmap over 24 months and named Meta (Dragonfly C1000 CPUs) and Microsoft (High Bandwidth Compute for Azure) as committed customers, targeting more than $15 billion in data-center revenue by fiscal 2029. The Street responded with a wave of target hikes — Morgan Stanley to $231, Rosenblatt to $265, Bernstein to $235 — marking a structural shift for a holding long valued as a handset business.

Lockheed’s defense tailwind. The White House meeting with defense primes, paired with the supplemental funding request and Lockheed’s plans to triple Patriot and quadruple THAAD interceptor output, reaffirmed the multiyear procurement framework underpinning the position regardless of the Iran ceasefire.
