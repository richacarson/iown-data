The Session

The ceasefire delivered. President Trump’s announcement of a two-week suspension of strikes against Iran—paired with Tehran’s agreement to coordinated reopening of the Strait of Hormuz—resolved the compressed binary that had defined markets for six weeks. The S&P 500 surged 2.52% to 6,783, the Dow gained 2.85%, and the Nasdaq rose 2.80%. The Russell 2000 led at +2.97%. The VIX fell 18.4% to 21.04.

The mechanism was oil. WTI crude collapsed roughly 17% to approximately $93 per barrel, and Brent fell about 14% to $94—the largest single-day decline in six years. The risk premium that had been baked into energy, inflation expectations, and rate policy unwound in a single session. CME FedWatch odds of a December rate cut jumped to 43% from 14% before the ceasefire. Treasury yields fell, with the 10-year declining approximately 9 basis points to 4.25%.

The rally was broad but not indiscriminate. Semiconductors and industrials led—names levered to global trade normalization and the AI infrastructure buildout that the crisis had overshadowed. Energy was the sole declining sector as the geopolitical premium compressed. Within the portfolio, the split was clean: rate-sensitive dividend holdings and growth-oriented tech rallied sharply, while the energy sleeve gave back crisis gains.

The S&P 500 at 6,783 remains below its 2026 high, and the ceasefire is two weeks, not peace. The portfolio was built for this—cash-generative businesses and structural demand do not reset because a truce clock starts. Stay the course.
