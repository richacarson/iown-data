The Session

The recovery continued, and then it consolidated. The S&P 500 added 16 points to close at 7,039.37, a new record and its third straight record close. The gain of 0.23% was the smallest of the recovery so far—a marked change of pace from the 10% rip that produced it. The Nasdaq closed up 0.36% at 24,102.70, also a record, and extended its winning streak to twelve consecutive sessions. That is the longest run for the index since July 2009. The Dow gained 0.24% to 48,578.73. The Russell 2000 added 0.22% to 2,719.60. Breadth narrowed but did not deteriorate; the tape moved from urgency to maintenance.

The bid arrived through semiconductors. AMD gained 7.80% to $278.26 on its twelfth consecutive up day, a streak it has not had in more than twenty years. Bernstein SocGen and other shops raised long-term revenue targets on the MI450 ramp and the Meta partnership. TSMC, which manufactures the AMD silicon, reported a 58% jump in Q1 profit overnight and guided full-year revenue growth above 30%. The number cleared every expectation. The stock declined 3.13% anyway, a classic sell-the-news response after a 147% twelve-month run and with capital expenditure guidance toward the high end of the prior $52–$56 billion range. AMD received the full benefit of the TSMC read-through; TSMC did not.

The macro data supported the tape. Initial jobless claims fell 11,000 to 207,000 for the week ending April 11, the largest weekly decline since February and below the 215,000 consensus. Continuing claims rose 31,000 to 1.82 million, a modest offset. The labor market is cooling in hiring but not in firing, which is the environment corporate America has been operating under for most of this expansion. Oil reversed higher as Strait of Hormuz concerns resurfaced; WTI traded above $93 and Brent approached $100 as the market weighed a Thursday-start ten-day Israel-Lebanon ceasefire against the ongoing US naval blockade of Iranian ports. VLO captured the refined-products lift, gaining 2.87%.

The Morning Brief framed today’s question as whether the market could sustain all-time highs now that it has found them. The answer on day one is yes, with composure. When the gains are small and the streak is long, the trend is doing the work. Think Like an Owner.

Holdings News

ABT: Q1 2026 earnings. Revenue of $11.16 billion exceeded the $10.99 billion consensus; adjusted EPS of $1.15 matched expectations. Sales grew 7.8% on a reported basis, 3.7% on a comparable basis. Medical Devices grew 13.2% on reported and 8.5% on comparable, led by Electrophysiology, Heart Failure, and Rhythm Management. The full-year adjusted EPS range moved to $5.38–$5.58 from $5.55–$5.80, incorporating $0.20 of dilution from the Exact Sciences close. Exact Sciences is expected to add approximately $3 billion of incremental sales in 2026. CEO Robert Ford framed the transaction as accelerating long-term growth while acknowledging the near-term earnings drag. The 6% decline reflects the guidance delta, not the operational quarter.

TSM: Q1 2026 earnings. Revenue of $35 billion (NT$1.134 trillion) and net income of NT$572.48 billion both exceeded estimates—the fourth consecutive quarter of record profits. Advanced chips were approximately 75% of wafer revenue. The company guided Q2 revenue of $39–$40.2 billion (10% sequential growth) and full-year revenue growth above 30% in US dollars. Capital expenditure for 2026 is now trending to the upper end of the previously guided $52–$56 billion range. Management said supply continues to run tight against AI chip demand. The operational read is entirely positive. The stock’s decline is a capital-discipline question, not an execution one.

Not held, but read-through: NFLX beat. Reported Q1 EPS of $1.23 versus the $0.76 consensus, a 62% beat. Not an IOWN holding, but the result confirms the strength of the US consumer on streaming and advertising—a constructive read for the portfolio’s consumer-discretionary and advertising-adjacent exposures.
