The Session

The week’s chip anxiety resolved on Friday into a genuine rotation rather than a retreat. The S&P 500 closed –1.01% at 7,457.78 and the Nasdaq –1.44%, yet beneath the index the money did not leave the market so much as change addresses. Semiconductors did the damage; energy, utilities, insurers and staples caught the bid. The Dow held better at –0.77%, and the VIX rose roughly 10% to 18.4 — elevated, but well short of panic.

The fresh catalyst crystallized a question the tape has been circling all week. Chinese startup Moonshot released Kimi K3, a powerful open model competitive with leading U.S. systems, and the market read it not as a demand story but as a cost one: if capable models can be built for less, the return on the industry’s enormous AI capital-spending plans becomes the debate. That reframing, layered on the week’s earlier reaction to a record TSMC quarter, pushed the semiconductor complex into bear-market territory — down roughly 20% from its peak, per Bloomberg. Nvidia slipped about 2% and briefly ceded the world’s-most-valuable-company title to Apple intraday. Netflix, which we do not own, added to the mood with a near 11% drop on soft third-quarter guidance.

The other side of that trade belonged in part to us. Renewed U.S.–Iran hostilities and fresh Strait of Hormuz tension lifted Brent — up roughly 16% from its July 1 low — and energy has been the strongest sector of the past week. Chevron (+1.91%) and Vistra (+1.89%) led the Growth Portfolio’s gainers even as the sleeve’s semiconductor and digital-asset weighting absorbed the day’s pressure, closing –1.09% against IUSG’s –1.34%. The Dividend Strategy — anchored in healthcare, staples, financials and insurance — nearly held flat at –0.55%, edging past SPY (–1.01%) while trailing a resilient DVY (–0.39%).

A bear market in one sector is not a bear market in a portfolio. Owning durable cash flows alongside secular growth is precisely what lets a –1% tape land as –0.55% in the sleeve built to defend it. We own businesses, not the ticker — and a rotation that pays our value names while our growth names catch their breath is the barbell working as designed.

Holdings News

Lockheed Martin (LMT) — $10.5B special-operations logistics award. The Department of War named Lockheed prime contractor on the Special Operations Forces Global Logistics Support Services II (GLSS2) program — a $10.5 billion, 12-year contract and USSOCOM’s largest service-contract vehicle, a competitive follow-on to work Lockheed has run since 2010. Separately, the company took a $1.6 billion Navy order for F-35 spares and committed $100 million to a European venture allocation alongside a new London office. Shares eased –0.93% with the broad tape despite the news.

Stryker (SYK) — Mako RPS launch. Stryker introduced Mako RPS, extending its flagship robotic-surgery platform further into knee procedures. The launch broadens the Mako franchise’s addressable base, though the stock traded lower with the medical-technology group on the day.
