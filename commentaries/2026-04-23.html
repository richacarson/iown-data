The Session

Wednesday’s records were rented, not owned. The S&P 500 closed Thursday at 7,108.04, off 0.42% from yesterday’s all-time high after touching another intraday record at the open. The Nasdaq led the give-back at –0.89% to 24,437, the Dow shed 0.36% to 49,310, and the Russell 2000 fell 0.37%. Two narratives ran in parallel: a sharp, sector-specific rout in enterprise software and a positive reset across analog semiconductors. The dispersion under a quiet headline made today a stock-picker’s tape.

The software break was the day’s defining event. ServiceNow fell roughly 17% after flagging a 75 basis-point subscription-revenue hit from on-premise deal slippage in the Middle East tied to the Iran conflict. IBM dropped about 9% on a print that beat top and bottom but maintained guidance the market was no longer willing to accept at face value. The contagion was indiscriminate—Salesforce, HubSpot, Workday, and Adobe all fell roughly 7–10%. Investors are repricing the entire SaaS complex against AI-disruption risk that has now moved from thesis to tape. The S&P Software & Services index dropped over 4% on the session.

Texas Instruments’ Wednesday-night print provided the counterweight. Q1 revenue of $4.83 billion (+19% year over year) and a Q2 guide of $5.00–$5.40 billion against a $4.7 billion Street were a clean read on industrial and data-center analog demand. TXN itself rallied roughly 18%, and the read-through carried the analog peers—ADI gained 5.89% and NXPI 6.83% in IOWN. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index added approximately 2% to a record, even as a Yahoo report noted the chip complex has added $3 trillion in market value and is the most overextended in decades—a tension worth holding in view.

Macro stayed live. Brent crude spiked above $105 after Israeli reports that Iran’s parliament speaker resigned from the U.S. negotiating team, and Iranian air defenses engaged unidentified targets over Tehran. Oil’s midday surge weighed on equities into the close. President Trump confirmed there is no deadline on the war or for Tehran to respond to his peace proposal, while Iran’s president said the U.S. blockade of Iranian ports remains the principal obstacle to talks. The ceasefire is holding nominally; the path beyond it is not clearing.

Dispersion days reward the discipline of owning real businesses across sleeves rather than concentrating in any single narrative. The dividend sleeve absorbed the software damage; the growth sleeve absorbed the rate-sensitive utility rally. Both worked.

Holdings News

NEE: Q1 Adjusted EPS $1.09, Up 10% YoY, Targeting High End of 2026 Guide. Florida Power & Light added nearly 100,000 retail customers over the trailing twelve months and posted 3.4% retail sales growth. FPL capex was $3.2 billion in the quarter against full-year 2026 guidance of $12–$13 billion. Energy Resources added a record 4 GW of renewables and storage origination including 1.3 GW of storage. Long-term EPS growth target reaffirmed at 8%+ annually through 2032–2035. The print is a clean validation of the rate-sensitive utility sleeve.

LMT: Cash Flow Turned Negative; Aeronautics Took $180M in Charges. First-quarter free cash flow was negative $291 million versus positive $955 million a year ago, attributed to working-capital timing and ERP-implementation effects. F-16 development delays and C-130 deliveries weighed on Aeronautics operating profit, which fell 14% to $619 million. Full-year revenue guide of $78.75 billion at the midpoint and EPS guide of $29.80 reaffirmed. The market read the print as confirmation that the production-cycle constraints the bears flagged are real and persistent.

ORI: Underwriting Quality Eroded; Combined Ratio Deteriorated 290 bps. Specialty Insurance pretax operating income dropped 47% year over year on a higher loss ratio and reduced favorable prior-year reserve development. Title Insurance improved with premiums up 12%. Net operating income per share of $0.68 versus $0.81 a year ago. Net premiums and fees grew 7% to $1.97 billion, but the quality of underwriting is deteriorating—the headline beat on revenue did not offset what the combined ratio said about the underlying business.

SSNC: Closed Down 0.88% After Yesterday’s Jefferies Target Trim. SS&C Technologies held up materially better than the broader software complex (which lost 4–15% across major names) thanks to its fund-administration business model being structurally less exposed to the AI-disruption thesis hitting SaaS. The relative strength is informative.
