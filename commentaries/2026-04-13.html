The Session

The market opened lower and then reversed course entirely, finishing near session highs. Futures had positioned for a difficult day — the Strait of Hormuz blockade took effect at 10 AM ET, oil surged overnight, and the Islamabad talks ended without an agreement. But Trump posted midmorning that Iran had reached out seeking a deal, and that single signal was enough. The S&P 500 finished at 6,885, up 1.01%, reclaiming all losses from the start of the war. The Nasdaq led at +1.23%. Oil, which had spiked as high as $102 Brent overnight, settled near $99 by the close — paring the morning’s gains as the diplomatic signal tempered the supply-shock fear.

Software was the session’s standout trade. Oracle surged approximately 11% on AI momentum, and the software ETF had its best single session in nearly a year — a reversal of Friday’s breakdown below support that technical analysts are calling a false breakdown. Goldman Sachs opened strong on Q1 earnings but gave back gains after a fixed-income revenue miss, ending the day lower. Airlines declined across the board on elevated fuel costs and weekend flight disruptions. The session confirmed what the prior week had begun to establish: this market is trading the diplomatic signal, not the military one.

Q1 earnings season opens with Goldman’s mixed print. Second-highest quarterly profit on record, but fixed-income trading fell short of expectations. The broader question for the week — whether war-era uncertainty will compress guidance — will sharpen as major banks report through Wednesday.

The S&P has now recovered to its pre-war level. The portfolio’s construction through this period — defense, energy, semis, and digital assets — was built for exactly this kind of volatile-but-resolving environment. Positions were never the question. Patience was.

Holdings News

SYK — Stryker acquires Amplitude Vascular Systems. Stryker signed a definitive agreement to acquire AVS, a Boston-based developer of an intravascular lithotripsy platform using pulsed CO₂ pressure waves to treat calcified peripheral arterial disease. Terms were not disclosed. CEO Kevin Lobo cited the deal as expanding the company’s peripheral vascular platform into a fast-growing cardiovascular procedure. The stock rose 2.39%.

FAST — Q1 earnings in-line; gross margin pressure drove the sell-off. Revenue of $2.2B and EPS of $0.30 both matched consensus. Gross margin contracted from 45.1% to 44.6% on unfavorable price/cost dynamics. Digital channels now represent 61.5% of quarterly sales; FMI device agreements grew 8% year-over-year. The industrial distribution franchise is performing; premium valuation left little room for in-line.
