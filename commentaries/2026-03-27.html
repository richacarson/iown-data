The Session

The deadline moved. The market didn’t. President Trump extended his power-plant strike ultimatum by ten days to April 6, framing it as a diplomatic concession after Iran allowed ten Pakistani-flagged oil tankers through the Strait. Futures briefly lifted on the Thursday-evening announcement, but Friday’s session erased any optimism. The S&P 500 fell 1.57% to 6,375.66—a seven-month low—completing its fifth consecutive weekly decline, the longest losing streak since 2022. The Dow shed 1.73% and confirmed correction territory, joining the Nasdaq 100 which crossed the threshold earlier in the week.

Brent crude settled at $112.57, up 4.22% and the highest close since July 2022. WTI briefly touched $100 before settling at $99.64. Rystad Energy noted the global system has shifted from “buffered to fragile” after nearly 500 million barrels of cumulative supply losses since the Strait closed. The VIX surged 13.16% to 31.05, its second straight session above 30. Ten-year Treasury yields climbed as bonds again offered no refuge, and futures traders pushed the probability of a Fed rate hike by year-end above 50% for the first time—a measure of how thoroughly the stagflation narrative has taken hold.

Gold was the session’s surprise. After falling 3.76% on Thursday in what appeared to be a confirmed inverse-to-oil pattern, gold reversed sharply on Friday, gaining 2.66% to approximately $4,492. The reversal complicates the thesis that escalation automatically compresses gold; today’s move suggests the metal can rally alongside oil when risk-off flows are broad enough. AEM gained 3.11% and GFI surged 4.54%, recovering most of Thursday’s losses. The digital asset sleeve continued lower: Bitcoin fell 3.98% to $66,035, IBIT declined 3.66%, and the miners tracked BTC with HUT down 6.28% and MARA down 6.53%.

Only energy, materials, and utilities escaped the selloff. VLO added 2.49%, CVX gained 1.62%, and CNX rose 1.42%. Among defensives, CHD advanced 1.58% and BKH added 0.34%. Tech bore the brunt: cybersecurity names fell sharply after reports that Anthropic is testing an advanced new AI model, with FTNT declining 3.49% alongside CrowdStrike and Palo Alto Networks each losing more than 5%.

The S&P 500 is now approximately 8% below its October peak—approaching but not yet at the –20% threshold where the first tranche of capital deploys. The energy sleeve continues to function as designed, providing ballast when the conflict intensifies. Patience is not passive—it is the strategy. Think Like an Owner.
