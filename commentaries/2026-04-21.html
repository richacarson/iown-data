The Session

The session ran the test the morning brief framed. Three discrete catalysts arrived in succession and the broad tape gave back ground: the Iran ceasefire expires tomorrow with Vice President Vance’s trip to Islamabad paused after Tehran failed to commit a delegation; Fed chair nominee Kevin Warsh signaled a “different, new inflation framework” in his Senate confirmation hearing, which markets read as an implicit hawkish tilt; and Apple announced Tim Cook will step down September 1, sending the index’s largest constituent down 2.5%. The S&P closed –0.66% at 7,063, the Dow gave back 0.59%, the Nasdaq fell 0.61%, and small-caps led losses with the Russell 2000 down 1.00%.

Crude reasserted as the Strait of Hormuz remained closed: WTI rose roughly 5% to $91.79 and Brent climbed back through $95, with Citi warning that prolonged Hormuz disruption could push Brent to $110. Gold reversed sharply, –2.71% to $4,697—a notable move on a risk-off day, reflecting dollar strength and profit-taking after the war-premium run. The VIX rose 7.53% to 20.29, registering the day’s headlines without breaking into stress territory. Bitcoin held relatively well at –1.08% to $75,486.

Both IOWN sleeves beat their benchmarks against the red tape. Dividend Strategy added 0.03%, beating both DVY (–0.44%) and SPY (–0.69%). Growth Portfolio finished at –0.08%, beating IUSG (–0.71%) by 63 basis points. The outperformance was concentrated rather than broad: STLD’s 5.19% advance on record Q1 steel shipments anchored the dividend sleeve, while AMD, FTNT, and HRMY led the Growth side. Energy holdings firmed on the crude bid—VLO +3.14%, CVX +1.49%—while gold-miner exposure (GFI –6.58%, AEM –6.15%) and crypto-exchange holdings (COIN –7.41%, HOOD –5.31%) accounted for the bulk of growth-side drag.

The morning brief framed today as a test of whether the post-March-30 recovery could absorb a stalled ceasefire, a hawkish Fed nominee, and an unexpected Apple succession. The broad index pulled back; the IOWN sleeves did not. Owning quality through a noisy session is the work.

Holdings News

SYF reported Q1 ahead of the open and the print was a clear beat that the stock didn’t reward. Synchrony posted $2.27 diluted EPS versus the $2.14 estimate, $805 million net income (up 6.3% year-over-year), record first-quarter purchase volume of $43 billion, and credit performance that came in stronger than expected. The Board approved a new $6.5 billion open-ended share repurchase program and a planned 13% dividend increase to $0.34 per share starting Q3 2026. The CET1 capital ratio stood at 12.7%. Despite the operational beat and the capital return announcement, the stock closed –1.21% at $77.63—a typical post-print profit-taking response after the recent run-up into the print.

STLD’s Q1 print delivered the operational story the morning brief flagged. Steel Dynamics reported $403M net income on $5.2B revenue with record steel shipments of 3.6 million tons. The headline EPS of $2.78 was a $0.06 miss versus the Zacks consensus of $2.84, but the operational metrics—73% sequential operating income growth, $86 per ton higher realized pricing, and continued aluminum-rolled qualification progress—were unambiguously strong. The 6% dividend increase and the 5.19% stock response confirm the market valued the volume and pricing trajectory over the headline EPS print.
