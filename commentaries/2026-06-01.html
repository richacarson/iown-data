The Session

June opened where May left off — at record highs, but the gain was narrow. The S&P 500 added 0.27% to close at 7,600.25, and the Nasdaq rose 0.42% to 27,086.81, its first close above 27,000. The advance, though, was concentrated: the Dow eked out 0.09% and the Russell 2000 slipped 0.47%, with breadth thin beneath the index prints. This was a tape carried by a handful of names rather than the whole field.

The catalyst was Nvidia. At Computex in Taipei, the company unveiled the RTX Spark PC superchip — a Windows-on-Arm processor set to ship this fall in machines from Dell, HP, Lenovo and others — and confirmed its next-generation data-center silicon had entered full production. NVDA closed up 6.26%, with Dell and HP both higher by double digits. The same news cut the other way for one of our holdings, a point we take up below.

The morning’s open question was whether the data would confirm the reacceleration that Friday’s regional surveys hinted at. It did. ISM Manufacturing printed 54.0 for May, a four-year high, above the 53.0 consensus and up from 52.7, with new orders climbing to 56.8 — a fifth straight month of expansion, anchored by AI-related capital spending. That keeps the “hot growth” read intact and reinforces the market’s expectation of no rate change at the June 16–17 FOMC, with the Fed blackout beginning June 6.

Underneath, the cross-currents were familiar. Volatility ticked up modestly — the VIX rose to 16.05 — while gold eased 1.74% to $4,513 and Bitcoin fell 2.86% to $71,486. Oil stayed firm as the Strait of Hormuz disruption kept supply tight and May U.S. crude exports ran at records; the proposed U.S.-Iran framework remained pending into the close, with markets leaning toward the peace-hope side of the ledger. Energy and the AI complex did the lifting; defensives and rate-sensitives lagged.

A record close on thin breadth is exactly the tape that rewards discipline over enthusiasm. With the index extended well above its long-term trend, our framework favors building cash patiently, not chasing the move. We own businesses, not the index.

Holdings News

Credo (Growth Portfolio) reports fiscal Q4 after today’s close. Shares eased 4.21% into the print as positioning de-risked; consensus sits near $1.03 in EPS on roughly $433 million in revenue. The report is the first supplier-side read on whether the AI-server backlog is translating into connectivity-layer fundamentals. We will assess the guide rather than the headline.

Qualcomm (Dividend Strategy) introduced its Dragonfly data-center brand at Computex, with further detail reserved for its June 24 Investor Day. The disclosure was overshadowed by Nvidia’s PC reveal, but it signals continued intent to diversify beyond handsets and into AI infrastructure.
