The Session

Friday answered the morning’s open question, and the answer was a rotation rather than a retreat. The chip cohort that reclaimed leadership Thursday on the memory through-print gave it back nearly in full: the spark this time was a reported plan by OpenAI to delay its public listing to 2027 rather than accept a valuation below the trillion-dollar mark, a headline that prompted the market to re-examine how durable the AI build-out’s spending commitments really are. The S&P 500 eased to 7,337.81 (–0.27%), the Nasdaq to 25,206.57 (–0.60%), while the Dow held near flat at 51,876.11 (–0.09%) and the Russell 2000 edged higher to 3,010.08 (+0.07%).

The internals told the more useful story. This was not broad distribution — the VIX actually fell 2.91% to 18.34, gold firmed to $4,086, and breadth outside of semiconductors was constructive. Capital rotated out of the most richly valued AI names and into the parts of the market that had been left behind: healthcare, payroll and insurance defensives, and — in a notable reversal of recent weeks — the crypto-equity complex, which firmed even as spot bitcoin sat quietly near $59,600. When the leadership reverses but the index holds and volatility recedes, the tape is reallocating, not abandoning.

For the committee, the read is straightforward. The two strategies finished modestly lower — Dividend Strategy –0.31%, Growth Portfolio –1.14% — with the gap explained almost entirely by the Growth sleeve’s heavier semiconductor weighting. The same names that flattered the book on Thursday weighed on it Friday. That is the nature of owning the leaders of a powerful theme: the ride is rarely linear, and a session that re-rates the speculative roadmap names is not the same as one that questions the underlying demand.

A rotation that leaves the index intact and volatility lower is the market doing our diversification work for us. We own the businesses, not the daily tape — and the dividend sleeve’s defensives did exactly what they are meant to do.

Holdings News

Qualcomm (Dividend Strategy). Wednesday’s investor day framed an ambitious pivot — more than $15 billion in data-center AI chip sales targeted by fiscal 2029 and a $40 billion non-handset revenue goal, with Meta and Microsoft named as early CPU customers. Shares spiked as much as 13–15% after hours, then surrendered the move over Thursday and Friday as investors noted the data-center silicon underpinning the target does not ship until 2028. The stock closed Friday at $189.39 (–7.57%). The roadmap is credible; the timeline simply leaves room for the market to re-underwrite it on risk-off days like this one.

Devon Energy (Dividend Strategy). Reports that Toms Capital has built a top-five stake open a potential value-realization track — asset sales, accelerated capital return, or portfolio reshaping are the usual activist levers. DVN closed at $42.21 (–0.92%) in a quiet energy tape. We will watch for a formal engagement letter or strategic-review headline as the next catalyst.
