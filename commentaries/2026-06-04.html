The Session

The chip selloff the morning set in motion arrived on schedule — and the broader market simply rotated around it. Broadcom fell roughly 13% after a Q2 beat that nonetheless left the Street wanting: management held rather than raised its $100B annual AI-chip target and signaled a pivot to selling chips alone rather than integrated systems. That was enough to reset a name priced for perfection, and the de-rating spread across the semiconductor complex.

It did not, however, spread to the tape. The S&P 500 added 0.41% to 7,584, the Dow climbed 1.73% — nearly 875 points to 51,561, just shy of its record — and the Russell 2000 rose 1.59%. The Nasdaq, down 0.07%, was the lone index in the red. Eight of eleven sectors advanced, led by Health Care (+3.14%), Financials (+2.67%), and Real Estate (+1.87%). The VIX fell 5.42% to 15.19, a reading that frames the day as orderly rotation rather than stress.

Crude reversed Wednesday’s war-premium spike, falling roughly 3% toward $93 as the U.S. reported an Israel–Lebanon ceasefire framework and President Trump pointed to possible progress with Iran this weekend. Gold held firm at $4,506 (+0.87%). On the data front, the revised first-quarter productivity reading was marked down to 0.3% from the 0.8% preliminary print — a modest cooling signal that sets up Friday’s May employment report as the week’s binding catalyst. Bitcoin slid about 4% to $63,231.

A single name’s valuation reset is not a verdict on the market. When breadth carries the tape while the most-crowded trade cools, ownership of the whole business — not the one headline — is what compounds. Avoid Erosion.
