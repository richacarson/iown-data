The Session

Iraq declared force majeure on all foreign-operated oilfields Friday afternoon, and the session broke. The S&P 500 fell 1.51% to 6,507, its lowest close since early September and its fourth consecutive losing week. The Dow shed 444 points (–0.96%). The Nasdaq dropped 2.01%. The Russell 2000 declined 2.26% and officially entered correction territory—a 10% drawdown from its recent high—making it the first major index to cross that threshold. The VIX surged 11.3% to 26.78.

The catalyst was unmistakable. Iraq’s oil ministry confirmed it could no longer ship crude through the Strait of Hormuz, with storage capacity at its limits. Brent closed at $112.19, up 3.3% on the day and nearly 9% on the week. WTI settled at $98.32. Drones struck two refineries in Kuwait overnight, adding to the supply picture. Saudi officials indicated crude could reach $180 if disruptions persist through late April. Citi raised its near-term Brent forecast to $120, with a $150 bull case.

The morning’s thesis on decelerating selling and paper-versus-physical divergence was upended by the Iraq force majeure—the de-leveraging did not exhaust before the next supply shock arrived. CBS reported the Pentagon is preparing to deploy ground forces, and speculation grew around a plan to seize Kharg Island’s oil export hub. Netanyahu said the war could end sooner than expected, but the market treated that as posture rather than substance.

Gold fell 2.0% to $4,513, posting its worst week since 1983. The forced-liquidation pattern continued: gold selling into a war that should be gold-positive, driven by dollar strength and margin calls overwhelming fundamentals. Bitcoin held relatively flat at $69,838, down just 0.12%—a notable divergence from the broader risk-off tone.

Energy was the sole sector in the green, gaining 1.5%. Real estate (–2.0%), utilities (–1.7%), and technology (–1.4%) led the decline. Today’s quadruple witching—the simultaneous expiration of trillions in derivatives—amplified intraday swings throughout the session.

S&P at 6,507. The –20% trigger at ~5,600 remains 14% below current levels. The Russell has entered correction; the Dow and Nasdaq are approaching the same threshold. Discipline over impulse—we do not act on sentiment, we act on structure. Avoid Erosion.
