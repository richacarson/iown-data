The Session

The session absorbed the weekend without breaking. The S&P closed –0.22% at 7,109, the Nasdaq gave back 0.26% to snap a 13-day winning streak that ranked as its longest since 1992, and the Dow finished flat. The Russell 2000 bucked the tape with a +0.58% session and a new closing record at 2,793. Futures pointed down 0.4% before the open after a weekend in which the US Navy seized an Iranian cargo ship and Iran fired at vessels while halting traffic in the Strait of Hormuz. The broad tape ended with a red tint, but the reaction function was measured rather than reactive.

Crude rebid the geopolitical risk that Friday’s session had unwound. WTI rose roughly 5% back above $86 and Brent climbed above $95, but both stayed well below the $100 level that defined the war-premium regime through March. Gold retraced 0.93% from Friday’s advance. The VIX ticked up to 18.87 (+7.95%), registering the weekend’s news without signaling stress. Bitcoin was the clearest outlier, adding 3.33% to $76,281 as digital-asset flows decoupled from the macro jitters.

Both IOWN sleeves finished higher against red benchmarks. Dividend gained 0.26%, beating DVY (+0.14%) and SPY (–0.20%). Growth Portfolio added 0.52%, beating IUSG (–0.42%) and SPY. The outperformance came from breadth, not a single beta trade: a Bitcoin miner, a steelmaker into its print, a semiconductor leader, a crypto exchange, and an auto-exposed chip name all contributed. Energy-linked holdings firmed modestly as crude rebid, but the positioning did not require a return to war-premium pricing to work.

The recovery from the March 30 low at 6,341 to Friday’s 7,101 print was a 12% advance in three weeks. Today was the first test of whether that pricing could absorb bad news. It did. One session doesn’t retire the question, but it establishes that owning the thesis through a weekend headline was the right trade.
