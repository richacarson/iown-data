The Session

The third quarter opened with a quiet index and a loud tape beneath it. The S&P 500 slipped 0.19% to 7,485.02, the Dow was essentially unchanged at –0.03%, and the Nasdaq gave back 0.64% — modest prints that concealed one of the sharper single-session rotations in months. The chip cohort that carried the best quarter since 2020 handed back a meaningful piece of its two-day surge, while the year’s most punished corners — crypto-adjacent names chief among them — caught a strong bid. Breadth ran positive for much of the day even as the cap-weighted indexes drifted lower.

The macro calendar delivered its first read of the second half. ADP private payrolls rose just 98,000 in June, below the roughly 110,000 consensus and down from 122,000 in May, with nearly half the gains concentrated in education and health services. Treasury yields, sharply higher early, round-tripped after the print — the two-year finished near flat at 4.15%. ISM Manufacturing came in at 53.3, a sixth consecutive month of expansion but an easing from May’s 54.0, with input prices still elevated. Fed Chair Warsh, making his first international appearance at the ECB’s Sintra forum, declined to offer forward guidance, said the Fed is “in the price stability business,” noted that labor markets are steady and inflation risks have come down, and previewed a “good family fight” at the July FOMC. Markets continue to price tightening rather than easing into year-end.

Trade policy added a second layer. The administration reportedly declined to extend the USMCA in its current form, opting to pursue separate arrangements with Canada and Mexico, and reports circulated of a push for a 15–20% minimum tariff on European goods. Tariff-exposed industrials absorbed the news poorly. In technology, a report that Meta is developing a cloud business to sell excess AI compute pressured AI-infrastructure names broadly — the question of whether hyperscaler capacity is becoming supply rather than demand landed on a cohort priced for uninterrupted buildout. Meanwhile the crypto complex, coming off its worst month on record for spot Bitcoin ETF outflows at roughly $4.5 billion in June, finally found its footing: Bitcoin touched below $58,000 overnight and reclaimed the $60,000 level, up 2.3% on the day.

The morning’s open question was whether the two-session chip extension would hold through the Warsh-ISM window or fade into month-open profit-taking. It faded, decisively — and at the same time the digital-sleeve divergence that had been running against the portfolio inverted in its favor. Leadership rotated; the ownership case did not.

A rotation this sharp inside a flat tape changes the leadership question without changing the ownership answer. We own both sides of the handoff — the chip franchises taking a breath and the laggards finding a bid. No tactical thresholds are in play.
