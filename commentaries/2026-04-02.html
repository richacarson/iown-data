The Session

Thursday opened as if the morning brief’s worst-case scenario had arrived. S&P 500 futures had fallen 1.3% overnight after President Trump’s primetime address promised weeks of continued strikes against Iran, and the index gapped down 1.5% at the open. The Dow shed more than 600 points in the first minutes. WTI crude surged past $113 intraday—briefly touching levels not seen since 2022—as the two-day ceasefire rally evaporated.

Then the reversal. Mid-session, Iranian state media reported that Tehran is working with Oman on a protocol to monitor ship passage through the Strait of Hormuz. The headline was enough. Equities ripped off their lows, with the S&P clawing from –1.5% all the way back to +0.09% at the close. The Dow finished down just 61 points, or –0.13%. The Nasdaq eked out +0.18%. The Russell 2000 led at +0.70%, a small-cap bid that typically signals positioning for domestic resilience over global exposure.

Sector leadership told the story beneath the index level. Energy, utilities, and real estate led—the trio you back when pricing for prolonged conflict and elevated oil. Consumer discretionary lagged, a direct read on the margin squeeze from $4.50 gasoline and the psychological drag of a war economy heading into a long weekend. WTI settled near $110, up roughly 10% on the day. Brent held above $108. The oil market is pricing continuation, not conclusion.

In the final hour, the White House announced 100% tariffs on patented pharmaceutical imports under Section 232, with carve-outs for companies that have signed MFN pricing agreements or committed to domestic manufacturing. The administration simultaneously adjusted steel and aluminum derivative tariffs to 25%. Neither announcement materially moved equity markets at the close, but the pharma order adds another layer of policy complexity heading into the long weekend.

The morning brief asked whether the session would confirm or deny the overnight selloff. The answer was decisive—equities rejected the lows and reclaimed the flat line. The portfolio enters a three-day weekend carrying event risk from Friday’s payrolls and Monday’s April 6 strike deadline, but the construction is designed for exactly this kind of gap.
