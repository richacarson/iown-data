The Session

The question the morning posed now has an answer. A tape printing fresh highs on the narrowest participation in weeks faced a binary outcome: leadership either broadens, or it mean-reverts. Tuesday broadened. The S&P 500 added 0.14% to 7,610.51 and the Nasdaq closed essentially flat at +0.03%, but beneath that placid surface the Russell 2000 led every major index higher at +0.90%, the Dow advanced 0.45% to 51,307.79, and the VIX eased to 15.73. Participation widened rather than narrowed.

Marvell anchored the session. Shares closed +32.52% after Nvidia’s Jensen Huang, in conversation with chief executive Matt Murphy at Computex, called Marvell “the next trillion-dollar company” and the firm unveiled its Teralynx T100 — described as the industry’s first 102.4 Tbps switch built for AI data-center networking. With Nvidia’s recent $2 billion investment and fiscal first-quarter revenue up 28% year-over-year, the endorsement landed on fundamentals rather than rhetoric. The move radiated across the connectivity and semiconductor complex: ADI +5.09%, QCOM +5.17% (erasing Monday’s PC-competition decline), LRCX +5.45%, KEYS +5.02%, NXPI +3.93%, and TSM +2.54%.

What distinguished Tuesday from recent sessions was where the strength extended. Caterpillar rose 5.14% as its power-generation franchise — the engines and turbines feeding AI data centers — continues to anchor a record order backlog. Steel Dynamics added 3.75%, PACCAR 3.12%, and Freeport-McMoRan 6.98% on the copper bid. The AI-infrastructure trade, long concentrated in the chipmakers, broadened into the industrials, materials, and power names that build and feed the buildout. Notably, Nvidia itself eased 0.69% — the leader rested while the rest of the field caught up, a characteristic signature of a broadening market.

The lone divergence was digital assets. Bitcoin fell roughly 6% to near $66,900, its weakest level since April, pressured by Strategy’s first disclosed bitcoin sale since 2022 and a record eleven-session stretch of spot-ETF outflows totaling about $3.45 billion. The committee’s digital-asset sleeve felt it directly — IBIT –6.03%, ETHA –4.83% — and the crypto-adjacent equities followed: COIN –4.72%, MARA –3.84%, HOOD –2.83%. Capital appeared to rotate out of crypto and into the AI and semiconductor complex, two sides of the same risk ledger.

On the geopolitical front, the tone softened from the morning’s framing. Reuters reported that Iran is studying a deal to halt the war and that talks are proceeding continuously, though Secretary of State Rubio clarified that no sanctions relief has been offered to reopen the Strait of Hormuz. Oil held a firm bid — VLO +2.27%, OKE +2.08%, CVX +0.93% — supported as much by the value rotation as by the residual war premium. Defense consolidated modestly, with LMT –0.59% and GD –0.47%.

A market that broadens is a sturdier market than one that climbs on a handful of names. Tuesday’s participation — value, industrials, materials, and chips advancing together — is the kind of foundation that rewards owning the whole portfolio rather than chasing the leader.
