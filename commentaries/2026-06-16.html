The Session

Monday’s chip-led advance found its other half on Tuesday, and the two sessions now read as a single round-trip. The same semiconductor names that powered the tape into the U.S.–Iran signing handed the gains back: the S&P 500 eased 0.55% to 7,512.99 and the Nasdaq gave up 1.14%, while a sell-off in the most-extended AI hardware names did the bulk of the work. This was the market clearing its throat before Kevin Warsh speaks.

The character of the day is best read in the dispersion. While the Nasdaq fell, the Dow Jones Industrial Average pressed to a fresh record close, and the proceeds of the chip sale rotated into value, industrials, and rate-sensitive cyclicals rather than into cash. That is the signature of a portfolio reshuffle, not a retreat. Our own sleeves captured the split with unusual clarity: the Dividend Strategy held within a whisker of flat at –0.16%, while the Growth Portfolio absorbed the chip reversal at –2.01%.

The backdrop is a Federal Reserve meeting that began Tuesday and concludes Wednesday at 2:00 p.m. Eastern, with Chair Warsh’s first press conference to follow. Futures price a hold at 3.50–3.75% as a near-certainty; the live questions are whether the statement sheds its easing bias and what the refreshed dot plot says about the path from here. With May inflation running at 4.2% year over year, a cohort of stocks priced for perfection chose to take a step back ahead of the answer. Crude continuing its slide toward $80, on expectations the Iran framework revives supply, pulled bond yields lower and gave the rotation its destination.

A record Dow alongside a falling Nasdaq is the market doing our diversification work for us. We do not chase the names that ran the hardest, and we do not flinch when they give a day back. The committee’s SPY $645.80 reference sits far below today’s tape; nothing in this session asks us to act. Think Like an Owner.
