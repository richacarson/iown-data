The Session

The catalyst the market had been waiting on arrived. Iran’s Foreign Minister announced on Friday morning that passage for all commercial vessels through the Strait of Hormuz was “completely open” for the duration of the Israel–Lebanon truce. President Trump confirmed shortly after. The response was immediate and emphatic: WTI crude fell roughly 11% to close near $84, Brent dropped more than 10%, and U.S. equities broke out to fresh record highs.

The S&P 500 closed at 7,125, up 1.19% on the day and nearly 5% on the week. The Nasdaq gained 1.52% and extended its winning streak to thirteen sessions—the longest since January 1992. The Dow added 1.79% to close above 49,400, and the Russell 2000 jumped 2.11%, a sign that the risk-on impulse reached well beyond mega-cap tech. The VIX settled at 17.48, a two-month low. For the first time since the Iran war began on February 28, the S&P 500’s war-related losses have been fully erased.

Beneath the index level, the session was a textbook rotation out of the war trade. Refiners and upstream energy names that had ballasted the portfolio through seven weeks of Hormuz closure gave back a share of those gains as the crude premium collapsed. Homebuilders, consumer credit, retail brokers, gold miners, and semiconductors led—every beneficiary of lower oil, lower inflation pressure, and a Fed with more room to cut. The IWS mid-cap value benchmark gained 1.31% on that mix; IUSG large-cap growth gained 1.44%. Our Dividend sleeve, with its refining and defense exposure, closed +1.15%—ahead of DVY (+0.18%) but trailing IWS on the day. Growth closed +1.12%, trailing IUSG as direct energy holdings (CVX, CNX, OKE) absorbed the rotation.

This is what we planned for. The energy sleeve was never the portfolio’s whole thesis—it was a structural hedge against exactly the scenario that has now begun to unwind. The cost of holding that hedge is a session like today, where those positions give back and our broader equity exposure doesn’t capture every dollar of the relief rally. The benefit is that we owned the exposure when it mattered most. Both outcomes are features of the framework, not failures of it.

The war trade is unwinding. The portfolio participated in the rally; the portfolio also kept the hedge that made the war weeks manageable. Own the thesis, not the narrative.

Analyst consensus still calls for Q1 EPS of $2.79, up 213% year-over-year.

Broader backdrop. Netflix (not held) fell more than 10% after weak Q2 guidance and the announced departure of co-founder Reed Hastings from the board. The move was the S&P’s largest single drag but did not impair the index’s advance. Hedge fund equity buying reached $86 billion this week per Goldman, the largest institutional weekly bid in months.
