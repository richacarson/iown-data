The Session

The pattern broke. For the first time this week, pre-market optimism held through the close. The S&P 500 gained 0.54% to 6,592, the Dow added 305 points (+0.66%), and the Nasdaq rose 0.77%. The Russell 2000 led at +1.23%, a broad-based risk bid that did not fade by midday.

The catalyst was familiar—a 15-point ceasefire proposal delivered to Iran via Pakistan—but the market’s willingness to hold the gap was new. Previous sessions saw peace-plan rallies evaporate by late morning. Wednesday’s held.

Oil’s response gave the rally structural support. Brent crude fell roughly 4% to settle near $96 per barrel, the sharpest single-session decline since Iran rejected the initial Hormuz mediation on March 18. WTI dropped to approximately $88. The pullback in energy costs eased inflation-premium fears and pushed Treasury yields lower—the 10-year retreated to 4.32%, down from 4.42% at Tuesday’s close. That combination of falling oil and falling yields gave equities room to breathe for the first time in a week.

Every sector except energy closed higher. Semiconductors led the session after Nikkei Asia reported that both AMD and Intel have informed customers of 10–15% CPU price increases amid worsening supply constraints. Delivery lead times have extended from one–two weeks to eight–twelve weeks, with some orders stretching to six months. AMD surged 7.26% to $220.27. Gold climbed 2.28% to $4,502, pulling miners sharply higher. Bitcoin recovered 2.22% to $70,956. The VIX fell 6% to 25.33.

Iran received the 15-point plan but has not formally responded. The IRGC’s counter-demands from earlier this week—US base closures, reparations, Hormuz tolls—remain the stated position. Goldman Sachs noted that crude is trading on the perceived probability of worst-case scenarios rather than the base case, and its base case still assumes Hormuz flows normalize in April. The five-day pause expires Saturday. The market is pricing hope; confirmation is still outstanding.

S&P 500 at 6,592 remains well within the hold zone. No trim or buy triggers active. The overnight de-escalation moved oil more than equities—watch whether Brent holds below $100 through the week.

Holdings News

AMD / Intel CPU pricing. Nikkei Asia reported both companies have notified customers of price increases across CPU product lines, effective March (Intel) and April (AMD). Average hikes range 10–15%, with some products seeing steeper increases. The shortage reflects AI-driven hyperscaler demand absorbing server CPU inventory. AMD’s EPYC Venice (2nm) is targeted for H2 2026. Structurally positive for AMD’s pricing power and revenue trajectory.
