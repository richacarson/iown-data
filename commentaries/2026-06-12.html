The Session

The recovery week closed the way it opened the rebound — with the market choosing resolution over fear. The S&P 500 added +0.49% to 7,430.86 for a third consecutive advance, the Dow gained +0.70%, the Nasdaq rose +0.31%, and the Russell 2000 led at +0.79%. The VIX compressed another 8.69% to 17.75, fully unwinding the early-week spike that accompanied the strike headlines.

The session’s centerpiece was the largest initial public offering on record. SpaceX priced 555.5 million shares at $135 Thursday night, raising roughly $75 billion, and opened Friday at $150 under the ticker SPCX. Shares traded as high as $176.52 before closing at $161.11, up 19.3% from the offer and valuing the company above $2 trillion. The much-discussed risk that the new supply would drain liquidity from the broader tape did not materialize at the index level — the rotation was confined to space-adjacent names, while the major averages absorbed historic SPCX volume and still finished higher.

The second pillar was the Iran track. A draft memorandum of understanding — reportedly committing the U.S. to lift oil sanctions and Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz — circulated through the session, with a signing possible as soon as Sunday in Switzerland. An administration official put the odds of an agreement in the coming days at 80%, even as the President disputed the leaked terms. Crude priced the progression: WTI settled down 3.2% at $84.88 and Brent fell 3.4% to $87.33, leaving oil roughly 6% lower on the week. That compression matters beyond energy — it directly unwinds the gasoline pass-through that drove Thursday’s hot May PPI print, easing the inflation question heading into next week’s June 16–17 FOMC meeting, where a hold is firmly priced.

Beneath the headlines, the cross-currents were constructive. Gold held near $4,200 as the People’s Bank of China disclosed a 19th consecutive monthly purchase, its largest single month since December 2024. Bitcoin steadied above $63,500, recovering from Thursday’s lows even as sentiment gauges remained deep in fear territory. Notably, the energy equities in our sleeves advanced despite falling crude — the market is beginning to price normalized operations over conflict premium.

No level was triggered this week. A week that began with war headlines ended with a peace window and the largest IPO on record — and we owned the same businesses on both ends. They finished higher.
