The Session

The record close did not survive the reopen. Monday delivered a chip-led record for the S&P 500 and a fresh high for the Dow above 53,000. Tuesday took much of it back. Samsung’s preliminary second-quarter numbers landed as a genuine triumph on paper — operating profit up roughly nineteen-fold from a year ago, comfortably ahead of consensus — and the market sold it anyway. The reading was not that the results were weak; it was that they were merely good after a group that had run so far, so fast that even a record beat could only clear the bar by a few points. That is what a crowded trade looks like when it pauses to catch its breath.

The give-back was concentrated exactly where the gains had been. South Korea’s Kospi fell nearly 5% and briefly halted trading; the U.S. semiconductor complex followed, with the broad chip ETFs off 5% or more and the memory and equipment names leading the retreat. Underneath it, a report that China’s DeepSeek is developing its own inference chip gave the tape a second reason to trim the highest-multiple positions. This is a reset of expectations, not of fundamentals — high-bandwidth memory remains tight, and the beat itself confirms the AI capital cycle is still turning.

What made the session instructive was not the sell-off but the rotation beneath it. As the AI trade cooled, capital did not leave the market — it moved. Energy caught a firm bid as Iran’s strike on a Qatari tanker near the Strait of Hormuz lifted Brent about 3% and the U.S. moved to revoke Iran’s license to sell oil. Utilities, staples, and healthcare drew the defensive flows. The result was a market that looked far calmer at the index level than the chip headlines suggested, with the Dow down a quarter percent while the Nasdaq shed more than a full point.

A diversified book is built precisely for a day like this — one where the leadership stumbles and the ballast holds. We do not chase the sleeve that is winning on any given Tuesday; we own both so that the portfolio, not the headline, sets the pace.
