The Session

The S&P 500 slipped 0.10% to 6,818 on Friday, ending its run just short of an eighth consecutive winning session. The tape opened higher after March CPI data landed roughly in line with expectations, but gains faded through the afternoon as traders positioned ahead of the weekend’s Islamabad negotiations. The Nasdaq outperformed, adding 0.34%, lifted by semiconductors: AMD surged 3.49%, NVDA gained 2.63%, TSM rose 1.40%, and LRCX added 1.89%. The Dow lagged, falling 0.56%, weighed by defensive names giving back some of the week’s relief-rally gains. The VIX settled at 19.33, down marginally.

The CPI report was the session’s main data event. Headline inflation jumped to 3.3% year-over-year, with a 0.9% monthly increase—the largest since 2022—driven almost entirely by gasoline, which surged 21.2% in March as the Iran war sent crude prices above $100. But the market found its footing in the core reading: core CPI rose just 0.2% month-over-month and 2.6% year-over-year, slightly below consensus. The divergence between headline and core tells the story the market wanted to hear: the energy shock is real but has not yet broadened into services or wages. The Fed’s path remains on hold—no cuts, but no hikes either—and that was enough to keep the tape from unraveling.

For the week, the S&P 500 gained more than 3%, its best weekly performance since November, with the Dow reclaiming positive territory for the year. The ceasefire announced Tuesday catalyzed the rally, and the market spent the rest of the week testing whether the optimism was warranted. Friday’s flat close suggests the answer is: cautiously, yes—but the real verdict arrives from Islamabad.

The S&P 500 has recovered roughly 7% from its March 30 low near 6,341 and reclaimed its 200-day moving average. The ceasefire rally has been constructive, but the portfolio remains positioned for sustained uncertainty. Islamabad is a framework negotiation, not a resolution—and the distinction matters for positioning.
