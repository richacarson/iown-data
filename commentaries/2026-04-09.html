The Session

The S&P 500 extended its winning streak to seven sessions, adding 0.61% to close at 6,824. It was a measured advance rather than Wednesday’s relief surge — the kind of follow-through that typically distinguishes a sustainable recovery from a one-day short squeeze. The Nasdaq gained 0.81%, the Dow added 0.58%, and the Russell 2000 climbed 0.60%. The VIX fell 7.3% to 19.50, settling back toward its long-term average for the first time since the conflict began in late February.

The session digested two crosscurrents simultaneously. On one hand, the ceasefire was visibly fraying: Iran’s parliamentary speaker accused Washington of violating three of Tehran’s ten conditions, Hezbollah claimed responsibility for overnight rocket strikes on northern Israel, and WTI climbed back above $100 as the Strait of Hormuz remained effectively closed despite the deal. On the other, economic data landed benignly: February’s core PCE matched expectations at 3.0% year-over-year, ticking down from 3.1% in January, while headline PCE held at 2.8%. The Q4 GDP third estimate came in softer. Jobless claims rose to 219,000, above the 210,000 forecast, suggesting a labor market that is cooling at the edges but not breaking.

The market’s ability to absorb rising oil, a deteriorating ceasefire narrative, and slightly soft labor data while still posting gains speaks to the positioning reset that Wednesday’s relief rally initiated. Short interest unwinds and systematic re-risking carried the index through the 200-day moving average at 6,651 and past the 50-day at 6,771, closing above both for the first time since mid-March. Israel’s announcement that it will pursue direct negotiations with Lebanon introduced a potential diplomatic channel that did not exist 48 hours ago, even as Beirut pushed back with “no negotiations under fire.”

The S&P sits 3.1% below its January high of 7,002. The ceasefire’s durability remains the near-term variable, but the seven-session streak confirms that participants are repricing toward normalization rather than escalation. Portfolios constructed around cash-generative businesses do not require predicting which outcome materializes.
